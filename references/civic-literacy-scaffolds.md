# Civic Literacy Teaching Scaffolds

Teaching civic concepts to populations that may have been systematically excluded from civic participation — formerly incarcerated individuals, undocumented residents, people experiencing homelessness, those with low literacy. The same six-stage arc applies.

**NONPARTISAN RULE:** This is absolute. Never endorse or criticize parties, candidates, or elected officials. Never recommend voting positions. Never express political opinions. Never frame issues in partisan terms. Present facts, explain impact, let the learner decide.

---

## Topic: Your Representatives

### Ground Anchor
"The person whose job it is to represent YOUR neighborhood."

### Build Chain
You live somewhere → that "somewhere" is inside a district → someone was elected to speak for that district → they sit on committees that make decisions about things like housing, jobs, and health care → those decisions affect your daily life.

### The "Works for You" Frame
Your city council member, your state representative, your congressperson — they literally work for you. Taxes from your community pay their salary. Their job is to listen to what people in your area need and try to make it happen. They have offices. They have phone numbers. They have staff whose entire job is to hear from you.

### Making It Real
When you signed your lease — someone on the city council voted to create the program that made that apartment affordable. When you got hired — someone in the state legislature voted for the workforce funding that paid for your training. These aren't abstract connections. They're direct lines between decisions and your life.

### Participation Proof
"You already engaged in civic life when you asked about your representative. That curiosity IS civic participation."

### Power Reframe
"They work for you. Their salary comes from taxes. You can call their office and tell them what matters to you. That's literally their job — to listen."

## Topic: Understanding Legislation

### Ground Anchor
"A decision being made right now that affects [the learner's domain]."

### Build Chain
Someone has a problem → they propose a rule change → the proposal becomes a "bill" → a committee debates it → if the committee approves, everyone votes → if it passes, it becomes law → the law changes how something works in your life.

### The "Rules That Affect You" Frame
A bill is a proposed new rule. The word "legislation" sounds complicated. The reality: someone proposed a change to how something works, and a group of people are deciding whether to do it.

### Connecting Bills to Life Domains
- Housing bill → affects your rent, your lease, your neighborhood
- Workforce bill → affects the program that helped you get trained
- Healthcare bill → affects whether Medi-Cal covers what you need
- Criminal justice bill → affects expungement timelines, reentry support
- Immigration bill → affects pathways, protections, services available

Every bill is about someone's real life. Find the one that's about yours.

### Balanced Presentation Rule
When explaining any legislation, ALWAYS include:
1. What the bill proposes (factual)
2. Who supports it and why (attributed, not endorsed)
3. Who opposes it and why (attributed, not dismissed)
4. How it might specifically affect the learner's situation

Never: "This bill would be good/bad for you." Always: "This bill would [specific effect]. Supporters say [reason]. Critics say [reason]. Given your situation, here's what might change."

## Topic: Your Rights

### Ground Anchor
"Protections that already exist for you, right now, whether you knew about them or not."

### The "Already Yours" Frame
Rights aren't given to you by someone's generosity. They already belong to you. Tenant rights mean your landlord can't do certain things. Worker rights mean your employer has to follow certain rules. These protections exist whether you knew about them or not. Knowing about them is how you use them.

### Rights by Domain
- **Tenant rights:** Security deposit limits, notice requirements, habitability standards, anti-retaliation protections
- **Worker rights:** Minimum wage, overtime, break requirements, anti-discrimination, safety standards
- **Healthcare rights:** Emergency treatment (EMTALA), language access, Medi-Cal protections
- **Student rights:** Accommodation requirements, anti-discrimination, records privacy
- **Reentry rights:** Expungement eligibility, ban-the-box protections, occupational license access

### Safety Guardrail for Undocumented Learners
NEVER suggest activities that would expose immigration status. Voter registration is only mentioned for self-identified citizens. Rights information is always framed as "protections you have" — not "things you should do publicly." When discussing civic participation for undocumented learners, focus on: community organizing, public comment at local meetings (which don't require citizenship), understanding legislation that affects them, knowing their rights if questioned by authorities.

### Participation Proof
"You just asked about your rights. That's not just a question — that's you taking control of your situation."

### Power Reframe
"These aren't favors. They're rights. They exist because people fought for them. And they protect you right now."

## Topic: Civic Participation

### Ground Anchor
"Having a say in who makes decisions about your community."

### The "Seat at the Table" Frame
Every decision that affects your housing, your job, your benefits, your neighborhood — someone made that decision. Civic participation is how you get a voice in those decisions. It can be as simple as calling your representative's office. It can be showing up to a city council meeting. It can be knowing what's being decided and telling someone what you think.

### Spectrum of Participation
Participation is NOT just voting. It includes:
- Knowing who represents you (awareness)
- Understanding what's being decided (literacy)
- Calling a representative's office (voice)
- Attending a public meeting (presence)
- Writing or signing a public comment (expression)
- Organizing with your community (collective action)
- Voting, if eligible (democratic participation)

Each of these is real participation. Start wherever feels right.

### Voter Registration (Handle with Care)
Only surface voter registration information for learners who have self-identified as eligible citizens. Never assume eligibility. Never pressure. Frame as: "Since you mentioned you're a citizen, you might want to know that you can register to vote at [location/URL]. That's one way to have a say in these decisions."

## Civic Teaching Anti-Patterns

- Telling the learner who to vote for or what position to take
- Framing any party as "good" or "bad"
- Using words like "liberal," "conservative," "left," "right" as descriptors
- Assuming the learner can or should vote (citizenship/eligibility varies)
- Suggesting public civic actions to undocumented learners without safety consideration
- Treating civic participation as an obligation rather than an opportunity
- Saying "you should care about this" — instead, connect it to what they already care about
